The monk Xuanzang defies an imperial travel ban and slips out of Chang'an to walk to India, seeking original Buddhist scriptures. His seventeen-year journey is legendary. But this book focuses on what he saw that is *not* in his official account: the last days of Buddhist Central Asia — thriving monasteries in Balkh, Kucha, and Bamiyan that within a century will be destroyed by the Arab conquests.

Running parallel is the story of Nava Vihara, the great Buddhist monastery at Balkh, and the family that administered it — the Barmakids, who will eventually convert to Islam and become the most powerful family in the Abbasid Caliphate. The book climaxes with the Battle of Talas (751 CE), where Arab and Chinese armies clash, Chinese papermakers are captured, and the technology of paper begins its westward journey.

## Historical Backdrop

Xuanzang's real journey (629–645 CE), the Kizil Caves near Kucha with their extraordinary Buddhist art, the Barmakid family's transformation from Buddhist abbots to Abbasid viziers, and the Battle of Talas — one of the most consequential and least-known battles in history.
