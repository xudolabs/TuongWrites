In 629 CE, the monk Xuanzang defies an imperial travel ban and slips out of Chang'an to walk to India, seeking original Buddhist scriptures. His seventeen-year journey is legendary. But this book focuses on what he saw that is *not* in his official account: the last days of Buddhist Central Asia — thriving monasteries in Balkh, Kucha, and Bamiyan that within a century will be destroyed by the Arab conquests. Xuanzang witnessed a civilization at its zenith that was about to be erased.

At the center of the story is Nava Vihara, the great Buddhist monastery at Balkh in present-day Afghanistan. Its towering white-plastered stupa with a gilded spire dominates the courtyard, prayer flags snapping in mountain wind, the snow-capped Hindu Kush rising behind it. The monastery's library holds manuscripts in Sanskrit, Sogdian, and Chinese on birch-bark scrolls and palm-leaf bundles. For centuries, monks have walked the circumambulation path around the stupa — the bricks grooved smooth by generations of footsteps.

Young Dara, grandson of the Pramukha (the abbot), tends the library with ink-stained hands. At twenty-two, his world is manuscripts, reed pens, and the careful work of preservation. He will live to be eighty-seven — long enough to watch his monastery fall, his family transform, and his grandson Khalid ibn Barmak become a powerful Muslim general who carries a hidden Buddhist text beneath his soldier's tunic.

Running parallel is the story of Khulan, a Tocharian woman artist working in the extraordinary Kizil Caves near Kucha, preserving Buddhist stories in paint as armies approach from the west. And Li Siyuan, a Tang Dynasty military cartographer who will be captured at the Battle of Talas and forced to reveal the secrets of Chinese technology.

The book climaxes with the Battle of Talas (751 CE), where Arab and Chinese armies clash in Central Asia. Chinese papermakers are captured, and the technology of paper begins its westward journey — while Buddhism begins its long disappearance from the region.

## Historical Backdrop

- **Xuanzang's real journey** (629–645 CE) and his descriptions of Buddhist Central Asia at its height
- **Nava Vihara monastery at Balkh** — one of the great Buddhist centers, eventually destroyed or converted
- **The Barmakid family** — their transformation from Buddhist administrators (*pramukha*, "abbots" in Sanskrit) to the most powerful dynasty in the Abbasid Caliphate
- **The Kizil Caves** near Kucha — extraordinary Buddhist art created by Tocharian artists, now fading
- **Kumarajiva's legacy** — the half-Tocharian translator who shaped Chinese Buddhism
- **The Battle of Talas** (751 CE) — where Chinese papermakers were captured and paper technology spread westward
- **The Tang Dynasty at its cosmopolitan peak** — Chang'an as the world's greatest city
- **The An Lushan Rebellion's early rumblings** — the event that would break the Tang

## Key Characters

- **Xuanzang** — historical figure, seen through the eyes of his fictional traveling companion
- **Dara** — young Barmakid monk at Nava Vihara, ink-stained hands, who watches his world collapse and must decide whether to convert, flee, or fight
- **Khulan** — Tocharian woman artist preserving Buddhist stories in the Kizil Caves as the armies approach
- **Li Siyuan** — Tang Dynasty military cartographer captured at the Battle of Talas, carrying secrets the Arabs want
- **Khalid ibn Barmak** — historical figure; Dara's grandson, broad-shouldered soldier with a reader's hands, who has become a powerful Muslim general while keeping a hidden Buddhist text
- **Anara** — Sogdian translator, merchant's daughter fluent in five languages, keeper of documents and artifacts, grey at the temples by forty

## Series Thread

*What happened inside Nava Vihara during the Arab conquests? How did a Buddhist abbot's family become the Islamic world's most powerful dynasty?*

The jade-bronze seal is found by Dara in the ruins of a Sogdian trading house in Balkh — connecting back to the merchant networks of Book 1. It passes to Anara, who guards it alongside the documents that preserve what the conquests tried to erase.
