In April 1720, on Île Sainte-Marie off the northeast coast of Madagascar, a dying Thomas Ashworth entrusts the Nomenclator to Adaeze — a formerly enslaved woman of Fon origin from Dahomey, freed when pirates captured her transport ship. With his final breath, Thomas passes on not just the cipher manuscript but a fragmentary clue within its pages: a reference to "the serpent bay" that may point to evidence of Libertalia, the legendary pirate utopia.

Six months later, Adaeze has carved out a place for herself on Île Sainte-Marie, using her linguistic skills to navigate between pirate and Malagasy communities. She has become obsessed with the Nomenclator's fragments and their hints about Libertalia — a place where, rumor says, pirates and formerly enslaved people built a free society, abolishing slavery and governing by consent a century before the Enlightenment.

But Adaeze is not the only one searching. Captain Sir Edmund Vance of HMS *Retribution* has arrived with orders from the Syndicate of Crowns — not to capture pirates, but to locate and destroy any evidence that Libertalia ever existed. For the colonial powers, an abolitionist pirate republic is not merely illegal; it is ideologically dangerous, a story that must never be told.

Armed with the Nomenclator and a treasure map assembled from its scattered clues, Adaeze journeys into Madagascar's uncharted interior — up rivers, through dense tropical rainforest, past stone ruins that may be natural or may be something else entirely. A treasure hunt through the jungle becomes a siege at sea, a confrontation with historical reality, and an answer to the question at the heart of the series.

## Historical Backdrop

Set in the twilight of Indian Ocean piracy, the novel explores real history and legend:

- **Île Sainte-Marie** — The pirate haven off Madagascar's coast
- **Libertalia** — The legendary pirate utopia, possibly fictional, reported by Captain Charles Johnson
- **Ratsimilaho** — Historical figure, the Betsimisaraka king of mixed Malagasy-pirate heritage
- **Abraham Samuel** — Historical pirate who reportedly ruled as a local king in Madagascar
- **The pirate settlements of Madagascar** — Real communities of retired pirates who intermarried with Malagasy people

The novel treats the Libertalia legend seriously while acknowledging its contested historicity — using fiction to explore the gap between what pirates dreamed and what they actually built.

## Series Thread

*The question becomes mythological: Did utopia ever exist?*

Adaeze adds testimony about whether noble dreams matter even if they fail. She entrusts the Nomenclator to a Chinese trader in the Madagascar–Pearl River network, connecting the story across the Indian Ocean to its final chapter in the South China Sea.
