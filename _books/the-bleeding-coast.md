In Jamaica's Blue Mountains, 1670, the Spanish deserter Alejandro Vega receives the Nomenclator from Kofi, a Maroon elder. The cipher book has traveled a remarkable path — from a Knight Hospitaller through the Benveniste merchant families to the Maroon communities of the Jamaican highlands. Now Alejandro carries it as he infiltrates Henry Morgan's privateer fleet in Port Royal, using forged credentials as a translator.

Alejandro's mission is personal: he is secretly searching for his kidnapped sister María somewhere in Panama. After impressing Captain Lawrence Prince and catching the eye of Morgan himself — who proves perceptive, charming, and dangerously shrewd — Alejandro secures a position as Morgan's personal translator. But as the expedition takes shape, he discovers hints of larger forces at work: "European investors" manipulating Caribbean politics, and the shadow network called the Syndicate of Crowns pulling strings from across the Atlantic.

The novel follows the real 1670–1671 campaign in harrowing detail: the assault on Porto Bello's three forts with a force of roughly 450 buccaneers, the siege of San Lorenzo, and the brutal nine-day jungle march to Panama City with 1,400 men — through impenetrable ceiba forests, across rivers, and into hallucination-inducing hunger. When Panama City finally burns, the plunder is bitterly disappointing: just 200–400 pesos per man, not the fortune Morgan promised.

From the forts of Porto Bello to the burning streets of Panama City, *The Bleeding Coast* is a story of brotherhood, betrayal, and the terrible cost of empire dressed as liberation.

## Historical Backdrop

The novel unfolds during Henry Morgan's documented campaign, with meticulous attention to historical accuracy:

- **Porto Bello** (July 1670) — Assault on three forts with approximately 450 men
- **San Lorenzo** (December 1670) — Siege of the fortress guarding the Chagres River
- **Panama City** (January 1671) — The nine-day jungle march and the burning of the city
- **Port Royal, Jamaica** — The "wickedest city on Earth," hub of English privateering

Historical figures include Henry Morgan, Governor Sir Thomas Modyford, and Don Juan Pérez de Guzmán. The novel corrects common myths: fire arrows become grenades and incendiaries, human shields become coerced ladder-carriers, and Morgan's political maneuvering is shown in its full complexity.

## Series Thread

*The question is weaponized: freedom as empire's tool.*

Alejandro adds a warning to the Nomenclator about how revolutionary movements get co-opted by empire-builders. The cipher book passes to Anne, a female tavern-keeper in Port Royal who runs an intelligence network. Her successor carries it to Nassau, where it awaits the next crisis of freedom.
