In the spring of 1809, Duarte Ferreira — a disgraced Portuguese naval officer fleeing a court-martial — is captured when his vessel is taken by the Red Flag Fleet. In the cargo hold, among plundered goods, he discovers the Nomenclator: a leather-bound cipher manuscript containing testimonies spanning 224 years, beginning with an entry from 1585 by "Alejandra de Santángel."

Duarte is brought before Ching Shih, the Pirate Empress. She commands the largest pirate confederation in history — the Red Flag Fleet of 800 junks and 25,000 people, the largest of six pirate fleets controlling the South China Sea. Her legal code is extraordinary: the death penalty for rape, equal shares of plunder, strict regulations on the treatment of captives, and a system of governance that functions as a floating nation.

As Duarte serves as a captive navigator, he begins decoding the Nomenclator's centuries of testimony. Reading Marcus Webb's entries about Nassau, he recognizes the parallels between Blackbeard's democratic ideals and Ching Shih's governed pirate society. A visit to Macau brings him to Samuel Benveniste — a member of the Sephardic merchant dynasty that has guarded the Nomenclator's secret across centuries — who cryptically warns Duarte about the choices faced by those who carried it.

The novel follows the real four-way power struggle between Ching Shih's fleet, the Qing Dynasty, the Portuguese navy, and the British East India Company. As Qing official Zhang Bailing issues ultimatums and amnesty negotiations begin, Duarte must make the final choice of the series: whether to record the truth about Ching Shih's confederation — that it is everything the previous Nomenclator writers dreamed of — or protect her secret from the Syndicate of Crowns, which has survived across centuries to crush exactly this kind of freedom.

The series finale answers the question the Nomenclator has been asking for 225 years: *"Can freedom exist outside the law?"* The answer is yes — but at what cost?

## Historical Backdrop

Ching Shih's Red Flag Fleet was the largest pirate confederation in recorded history. The novel follows real events:

- **Ching Shih's legal code** — Including the death penalty for rape, regulated plunder, and governance structures
- **The Red Flag Fleet** — 800 junks, 25,000+ people, controlling South China Sea trade routes
- **Zhang Bao** — Ching Shih's lieutenant and adopted son/husband, a complex historical figure
- **The amnesty negotiations** — The real diplomatic endgame between the pirates, the Qing Dynasty, Portugal, and Britain
- **Ching Shih's retirement** — One of the only major pirates in history to retire peacefully, keeping her wealth and status

## Key Historical Figures

## Series Thread

*The question is answered: Yes, but at what cost.*

The final Nomenclator entry. Duarte realizes Ching Shih's confederation is everything the previous writers dreamed of — freedom outside the law, functioning at a scale none of them imagined. The Nomenclator's fate is left ambiguous: hidden, preserved, waiting to be found.

The epilogue, set in 1823 Canton, is told from Ching Shih's own perspective — a woman who negotiated her way from pirate empress to respected citizen, running a gambling house in Canton, her secrets intact. The series ends with three words: *"She won."*
