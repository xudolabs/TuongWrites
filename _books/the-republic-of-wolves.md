In August 1716, Thomas Ashworth, a young English scribe, is shipwrecked in Jamaica. In a Port Royal tavern — rebuilt after the devastating 1692 earthquake — he receives the Nomenclator from Esther, its keeper, with a simple instruction: travel to Nassau and document what happens when pirates try to build a republic.

Thomas arrives in Nassau harbor to find something extraordinary. The settlement is chaotic, stinking, and lawless — but it is also the world's only functioning pirate democracy. The Flying Gang governs through democratic councils where every man votes. Ship articles guarantee equal shares of plunder, disability compensation, and elected captains. Twenty-five to thirty percent of crews are African or mixed-race sailors fighting as equals — a level of racial integration that won't be seen again for centuries.

Thomas drafts ship articles, witnesses council debates where Charles Vane advocates anarchic freedom, Benjamin Hornigold argues for sustainable piracy, and Blackbeard serves as a philosophical mediator. He encounters Marcus Webb, who appears to be just another pirate but harbors a dangerous secret. As Thomas documents the republic's brief, imperfect bloom, Woodes Rogers sails from England with a King's Pardon in one hand and warships in the other.

The novel follows every major historical event: the King's Pardon (September 5, 1717), Rogers' arrival (July 26, 1718), Vane's defiant fireship escape from Nassau harbor (November 23, 1718), Blackbeard's operatic last stand at Ocracoke, and Vane's austere execution (March 29, 1720). Each death carries a distinct tone — Blackbeard's is theatrical, Vane's is stoic, and the republic's is quiet, inevitable, and devastating.

## Historical Backdrop

The golden age of piracy's most explosive chapter, with every major event historically documented:

- **The Flying Gang** — The informal pirate confederacy at Nassau
- **The King's Pardon** — Issued September 5, 1717, for crimes committed before January 5, 1718
- **Woodes Rogers' arrival** — July 26, 1718, the beginning of the end
- **Blackbeard's death** — The legendary last stand at Ocracoke Inlet
- **Vane's execution** — March 29, 1720, hanged at Port Royal
- **The 1715 Spanish Treasure Fleet disaster** — The event that drew pirates to the Bahamas

## Series Thread

*The question becomes political: Can pirates govern themselves?*

Thomas documents the rise and fall of the republic — democracy's brief, imperfect bloom. He escapes Nassau's fall and flees to Madagascar, arriving sick with malaria. In April 1720, dying, he entrusts the Nomenclator directly to Book 4's protagonist, Adaeze, with his final breath.
