In 843 CE, the Tang Emperor Wuzong launches a catastrophic purge: Buddhism, Manichaeism, Zoroastrianism, and Nestorian Christianity are banned. 260,000 monks and nuns are forcibly returned to lay life. Temples are destroyed. Sacred texts are burned. But in the oasis city of Dunhuang, far from the capital, a network of monks, merchants, and scribes launches a secret operation to save the condemned texts.

Over the next 160 years, this hidden library grows to 45,000 documents in 17 languages — including the world's oldest printed book (the Diamond Sutra, 868 CE), star charts, medical treatises, and Manichaean prayers disguised as Buddhist texts. Around 1006 CE, someone seals the entire library behind a false wall in Cave 17 of the Mogao Caves. It will not be opened for 900 years.

## Historical Backdrop

The Huichang Persecution (843–845 CE), the real Dunhuang cave library, the Diamond Sutra, the Uighur Kingdom of Qocho where religions coexisted, and the Kara-Khanid conquest of Khotan that ended Buddhist Central Asia.

## Series Thread

*The jade-bronze seal is hidden inside Cave 17 with the manuscripts* — buried alive with the knowledge it has traveled alongside for eight centuries.
