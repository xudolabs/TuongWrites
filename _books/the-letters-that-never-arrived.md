In 97 CE, the Chinese emissary Gan Ying is sent by General Ban Chao to reach the Roman Empire — the farthest west any Chinese envoy has ever traveled. Accompanying him is Kharash, a Sogdian translator with bright brown eyes and a jade-and-bronze merchant's seal on a leather cord at his neck — the artifact that will connect all five books across sixteen centuries.

Their journey takes them from the garrison compound at Kucha, through the oasis kingdoms of the northern Taklamakan rim, past Buddhist monasteries with prayer flags snapping in the wind, and into Parthian territory. But at the shores of the Persian Gulf, Parthian sailors feed Gan Ying lies about the crossing — "the sea takes two years, and men die of homesickness" — and he turns back. The direct link between Rome and China that could have changed world history is broken by a single act of deception. The Parthians' reasons, the novel reveals, are more complex than simple greed.

Two centuries later, in 313 CE, a Sogdian merchant woman named Miwnay is stranded in Dunhuang, abandoned by her husband, destitute. As the Xiongnu sack Luoyang and the Western Jin dynasty crumbles around her, she fights to survive in a collapsing frontier town, keeping a sacred Zoroastrian fire burning with rationed juniper resin. She writes desperate letters home that will never be delivered. Her letters — real documents found in a watchtower along the Great Wall in 1907 — are among the most human artifacts of the ancient world. In one, she writes: *"I would rather be a dog's or a pig's wife than yours."*

Meanwhile, the merchant Nanai-vandak carries a letter from burning Luoyang, documenting the catastrophe in real time as he walks 489 days across a disintegrating empire, counting his goods even as civilization collapses around him.

The book braids these two timelines. Both stories are about messages that never reach their destination — and the world-changing consequences of silence.

## Historical Backdrop

Based on real historical documents and events:

- **Gan Ying's mission to Rome** (97 CE) — recorded in the *Book of the Later Han*, the farthest westward journey of any Chinese envoy
- **The Sogdian Ancient Letters** (313 CE) — real documents discovered in a watchtower in 1907, especially Letter #1 (Miwnay's) and Letter #5 (Nanai-vandak's account of the fall of Luoyang)
- **The lost city of Loulan** — still inhabited in this period, a critical waystation that will later be swallowed entirely by desert
- **The Parthian Empire's trade monopoly** — their strategic interest in preventing direct Rome-China contact
- **Han Dynasty frontier life** — watchtowers, garrison soldiers, desert crossings
- **Early Sogdian merchant networks** — the *sabao* (caravan leader) system

## Key Characters

- **Gan Ying** — Chinese emissary with deep-set eyes that miss nothing, a scar on his left forearm from a skirmish near Kashgar, military bearing masking growing disillusionment
- **Kharash** — Sogdian translator and merchant, multilingual chameleon who carries the jade-bronze seal, bright eyes masking deepening guilt
- **Miwnay** — Sogdian merchant's wife, fury expressed in her famous letters, merchant's arithmetic reflex calculating survival
- **Nanai-vandak** — Sogdian merchant documenting the fall of Luoyang, counting goods as the world burns

## Series Thread

*Where the jade-bronze seal's journey begins.*

Kharash carries the Sogdian merchant's seal — lion and dragon intertwined, inscribed in Sogdian, Parthian, and Chinese — on a cord at his neck. It enters the story as a merchant's credential and exits as a talisman, beginning its 1,356-year journey across the Silk Road.
