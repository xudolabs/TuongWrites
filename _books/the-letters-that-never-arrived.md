In 97 CE, the Chinese emissary Gan Ying is sent by General Ban Chao to reach the Roman Empire — the farthest west any Chinese envoy has ever traveled. But at the shores of the Persian Gulf, Parthian sailors feed him lies about the crossing, and he turns back. The direct link between Rome and China that could have changed world history is broken by a single act of deception.

Two centuries later, in 313 CE, a Sogdian merchant woman named Miwnay is stranded in Dunhuang, abandoned by her husband, destitute, writing desperate letters home that will never be delivered. Her letters — real documents found in a watchtower in 1907 — are among the most human artifacts of the ancient world. In one, she writes: *"I would rather be a dog's or a pig's wife than yours."*

Braiding these two timelines, this is a story about messages that never reach their destination — and the world-changing consequences of silence.

## Historical Backdrop

Based on real historical documents: the Sogdian Ancient Letters, discovered in a watchtower along the Great Wall in 1907. Gan Ying's mission is recorded in the *Book of the Later Han*. The lost city of Loulan — still inhabited in this period — will later be swallowed entirely by desert.

## Series Thread

*Where the jade-bronze seal's journey begins.*

Kharash, Gan Ying's Sogdian translator, carries the seal on a cord at his neck. It enters the story as a merchant's credential and exits as a talisman — the reader's thread through sixteen centuries.
