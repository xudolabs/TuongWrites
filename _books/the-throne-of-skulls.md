The Mongol conquests are the most destructive — and most transformative — event in Silk Road history. In a single generation, Genghis Khan annihilates the Khwarezmian Empire, erases the Tangut civilization of Western Xia, and his grandsons destroy Baghdad and its House of Wisdom. But in the ruins, the Mongols build the Pax Mongolica — the first and only time a single passport could carry you from Korea to Hungary.

The novel follows three converging storylines:

**The Destruction.** In 1227, as Genghis Khan's armies close in on the desert city of Khara-Khoto, a Tangut scholar named Sonam races through burning streets carrying armfuls of manuscripts. The Tangut script — 6,000 unique characters created to rival Chinese — will become unreadable for 700 years after this night. Sonam's desperate effort to preserve his civilization's literature is a race against total erasure. Among the artifacts he carries is the jade-bronze seal, recovered from its centuries of burial, once again in the hands of someone fighting to save knowledge from destruction.

**The Library of the Assassins.** In 1256, Yasmin — a young Ismaili woman trained in astronomy and alchemy — guards the legendary library of Hassan-i Sabbah at the mountain fortress of Alamut. The library holds astronomical texts, alchemical formulas, and the Old Man of the Mountain's personal writings. When Hulagu Khan's vast Mongol army besieges the fortress, she must choose what to save and what to let burn. The library's contents — what Hulagu specifically ordered destroyed and whether any of it survived — remain one of history's great mysteries.

**The Reverse Marco Polo.** In 1287, Rabban Bar Sauma — a Nestorian Christian monk born in Khanbaliq (Beijing) — travels *westward* across the entire Mongol Empire to meet the Pope in Rome, the King of France in Paris, and the King of England. He celebrates the Eucharist in Rome. He is the mirror image of Marco Polo — an Asian Christian visiting a bewildered Europe — and almost no one has heard of him. His journey reveals the Pax Mongolica at its most extraordinary: a world connected as never before, built on mountains of skulls.

## Historical Backdrop

- **The Mongol destruction of Khwarezmia** (1219–1221) — Merv, Nishapur, Balkh leveled, millions killed
- **The erasure of Western Xia / Tangut civilization** (1227) — an entire culture made unreadable
- **Khara-Khoto** — the Tangut desert city, its manuscripts buried in sand until rediscovered in 1908
- **The Assassin fortress of Alamut** and its legendary library, destroyed 1256
- **Hulagu's sack of Baghdad** (1258) — the end of the Abbasid Caliphate, the Tigris running black with ink
- **The Pax Mongolica** — cosmopolitan courts, religious debates, unprecedented connectivity
- **William of Rubruck's** account of the Karakorum debate (Buddhist, Muslim, and Christian theologians)
- **Rabban Bar Sauma's journey to Europe** (1287–1288)
- **Guillaume Boucher** — a Parisian goldsmith enslaved by the Mongols who builds the famous silver-tree fountain at Karakorum
- **Sorghaghtani Beki** — the Nestorian Christian Mongol queen who engineered her four sons onto the thrones of Asia

## Key Characters

- **Sonam** — Tangut scholar and keeper of the Khara-Khoto library; carries manuscripts into the desert as his city dies
- **Yasmin** — Ismaili woman trained in astronomy and alchemy at Alamut; the last guardian of Hassan-i Sabbah's writings
- **Rabban Bar Sauma** — historical figure, the Nestorian monk from Beijing who walks to Rome
- **Guillaume Boucher** — historical figure, the Parisian goldsmith enslaved by the Mongols who builds wonders at Karakorum
- **Sorghaghtani Beki** — historical figure, the Nestorian Christian Mongol queen, one of the most powerful women in medieval history

## Series Thread

*The jade-bronze seal is recovered by Sonam from Khara-Khoto* and carried through Mongol territory — surviving the most violent century in Silk Road history. It passes through the Pax Mongolica, witness to both the destruction and the extraordinary connectivity that rose from the ruins.
